"Triamcinolone Acetonide is a corticosteroid. It suppresses inflammation. In the right clinical context — eczema, psoriasis, localized swelling — it has legitimate uses.

The face is not that context.

Topical corticosteroids on facial skin, used daily over weeks and months, produce predictable and documented effects:

Skin atrophy. The dermis physically thins. Collagen production is suppressed. The structural layer that Tretinoin is specifically working to rebuild — corticosteroids reverse that work.

Steroid-induced hyperpigmentation. In darker skin tones, chronic corticosteroid use on the face is a documented driver of the exact problem I am trying to solve. The production line I am shutting down with Azelaic Acid — this reopens it.

Telangiectasia. Permanently visible small blood vessels from a thinned dermis.

Rebound dependency. Stop the corticosteroid after extended use, and the skin flares. This is how people end up locked into continued use without understanding why.

None of these effects are hypothetical. They are in the clinical literature. And they were being delivered to my face in a cream I was applying every single morning."

05 De Oplossing — The QAL Audit Protocol 4:30 – 6:30
Regie

Notitieboek open. Blader naar de pagina. GFX: "The QAL Audit Protocol". Dit is het praktische segment — de reden waarom mensen deze video aan anderen sturen. Duidelijk, stap voor stap, niks overslaan.

Script

"This is the protocol I now run every time I pick up a compounded prescription. Three checks. Takes sixty seconds. Non-negotiable.

Step one: Verify the active and the concentration.

Your prescription has a specific active at a specific percentage. Read the label before you leave the counter. Not at home. At the counter. If the concentration does not match what was prescribed — refuse it. Ask for the correct formulation.

Step two: Check for abbreviations you did not prescribe.

Compounded labels use abbreviations. Common ones to know: TA or TAC — Triamcinolone Acetonide, a corticosteroid. HCT — Hydrocortisone, a corticosteroid. BDP — Betamethasone Dipropionate, a strong corticosteroid. If any abbreviation appears on your label that was not in your original prescription — stop. Ask the pharmacist to explain every ingredient before you accept.

Step three: Compare against your original prescription on file.

Ask the pharmacist to pull your original prescription and read it to you. Every ingredient. Every concentration. If anything deviates — even by one percent — refuse the batch and request correction.

[pauze — blik direct naar camera]

You are not being difficult. You are auditing hardware that goes on your face every day. That is your right."

// The QAL Audit Protocol — GFX Reference
  1. Verify active + concentration — does the label match your prescription exactly? Check at the counter, not at home.
  2. Scan for unknown abbreviations — TA/TAC (Triamcinolone), HCT (Hydrocortisone), BDP (Betamethasone). Any unrecognized abbreviation = stop and ask.
  3. Compare against original prescription on file — request verbal confirmation of every ingredient. Any deviation = refuse the batch.
